1.12. Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors#

Eigenvalue problems arise in many situations, for example

  • calculating the natural frequencies of oscillation of a vibrating system

  • finding principal axes of stress and strain

  • calculating the oscillations of an electrical circuit

Definition 1.21 (Eigenvalue Problem)

Find all the values of \(\lambda\) for which the equation

\[\begin{equation*} {\bf{A}}\boldsymbol{x} = \lambda \boldsymbol{x} \tag{1.34} \end{equation*}\]

has a non-zero solution \(\boldsymbol{x}\), where \({\bf{A}}\) is an \(n\times n\) matrix and \(\boldsymbol{x}\) is an \(n\times 1\) matrix (column vector).

Such values of \(\lambda\) are called eigenvalues of the matrix \({\bf{A}}\), and the corresponding vectors, \(\boldsymbol{x}\), are called the eigenvectors.

Equation (1.34) may be written as

\[\begin{eqnarray*} && \qquad\ \ {\bf{A}}\boldsymbol{x} &=& \lambda {\bf{I}}\boldsymbol{x} \\ \Leftrightarrow &\quad& {\bf{A}}\boldsymbol{x} - \lambda {\bf{I}}\boldsymbol{x} &=& {\bf{0}} \\ \Leftrightarrow && \left({\bf{A}} - \lambda {\bf{I}}\right)\boldsymbol{x} &=& {\bf{0}} \tag{1.35} \end{eqnarray*}\]

The symbol “\(\Leftrightarrow\)” means “if and only if”, i.e., the rows in (1.35) are equivalent.

If \(\textrm{det}\left({\bf{A}} - \lambda{\bf{I}}\right)\ne0\), the matrix \({\bf{A}} - \lambda{\bf{I}}\) may be inverted and the unique solution to (1.35) is

\[\begin{equation*} \boldsymbol{x} = \left({\bf{A}} - \lambda{\bf{I}}\right)^{-1}{\bf{0}} = {\bf{0}}. \end{equation*}\]

Non-zero solutions to (1.35) for \(\boldsymbol{x}\) will only exist if

\[\begin{equation*} \textrm{det}\left({\bf{A}} - \lambda{\bf{I}}\right) = 0. \tag{1.36} \end{equation*}\]

There are infinitely many eigenvectors corresponding to any given eigenvalue.

This is because: if \(\boldsymbol{x}\) is an eigenvector of \({\bf{A}}\) corresponding to the eigenvalue \(\lambda\) and \(c\) is a non-zero scalar, then \(c\boldsymbol{x}\) is also an eigenvector of \({\bf{A}}\). To see this note

\[\begin{equation*} {\bf{A}}\left(c\boldsymbol{x}\right) = c{\bf{A}}\boldsymbol{x} = c\lambda\boldsymbol{x} = \lambda\left(c\boldsymbol{x}\right). \end{equation*}\]

Eigenvalues of \(2\times2\) Matrices#

If \({\bf{A}}\) is the \(2\times 2\) matrix \(\displaystyle {\bf{A}} = \left(\begin{array}{cc} a & b \\ c & d \end{array}\right)\) then (1.36) is a quadratic equation for \(\lambda\).

We can see this by calculating

\[\begin{eqnarray*} \left|{\bf{A}} - \lambda{\bf{I}}\right| &=& \left|\left(\begin{array}{cc} a & b \\ c & d \end{array}\right) - \lambda \left(\begin{array}{cc} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{array}\right)\right| \\ &=& \left|\begin{array}{cc} a - \lambda & b \\ c & d - \lambda \end{array}\right| \\ &=& \left(a - \lambda\right)\left(d - \lambda\right) - bc \\ &=& \lambda^2 - \left(a + d\right)\lambda + \left(ad - bc\right). \end{eqnarray*}\]

For \(\lambda\) to be an eigenvalue of \({\bf{A}}\) we require

\[\begin{equation*} \lambda^2 - \left(a + d\right)\lambda + \left(ad - bc\right) = 0. \end{equation*}\]

We can factorize, complete the square, or use the standard formula for solving a quadratic equation to obtain the eigenvalues.

The Characteristic Equation#

Definition 1.22 (The Characteristic Equation)

If \({\bf{A}}\) is an \(n\times n\) matrix then \(\textrm{det}\left({\bf{A}} - \lambda{\bf{I}}\right) \) is a polynomial of degree \(n\) in \(\lambda\), called the characteristic polynomial. The characteristic equation is

\[\begin{equation*} \textrm{characteristic polynomial} = 0, \end{equation*}\]

i.e.,

\[\begin{equation*} \lambda^n + c_{n-1}\lambda^{n-1} + c_{n-2}\lambda^{n-2} + \cdots + c_1\lambda + c_0 = 0. \tag{1.37} \end{equation*}\]

The coefficients \(c_0\), \(c_1\), \(\ldots\), \(c_{n-1}\) can be found once the elements of \({\bf{A}}\) are known.

The characteristic equation has \(n\) roots (solutions of (1.37)) \(\lambda_1\), \(\lambda_2\), \(\ldots\), \(\lambda_n\). Some of these roots may be equal, in which case they are said to be repeated roots.

Example 1.24

Find the eigenvalues and corresponding eigenvectors of the matrix

\[\begin{equation*} {\bf{A}} = \left(\begin{array}{rr} 3 & -2 \\ 4 & -3 \end{array}\right). \end{equation*}\]

Solution

  • Step 1
    Find the characteristic polynomial

\[\begin{eqnarray*} \textrm{det}\left({\bf{A}} - \lambda{\bf{I}}\right) &=& \left|\begin{array}{cc} 3 - \lambda & -2 \\ 4 & -3 - \lambda \end{array}\right| \\ &=& \left(3 - \lambda\right)\left(-3 - \lambda\right) + 8 \\ &=& \lambda^2 - 9 + 8 = \lambda^2 - 1 \end{eqnarray*}\]

  • Step 2
    Solve the characteristic equation

\[\begin{equation*} \lambda^2 - 1 = 0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad \lambda = -1~\textrm{or}~\lambda = 1. \end{equation*}\]

  • Step 3
    For each eigenvalue find the corresponding eigenvector.

    • Case \(\lambda = -1\):
      Write out
      \(\displaystyle \qquad\qquad\qquad \left({\bf{A}} - \lambda{\bf{I}}\right)\boldsymbol{x} = {\bf{0}} \)
      in full to give

    \[\begin{eqnarray*} &\left[\left(\begin{array}{rr} 3 & -2 \\ 4 & -3 \end{array}\right) + \left(\begin{array}{cc} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{array}\right)\right]\left(\begin{array}{c} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{array}\right) &=& \left(\begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 0\end{array}\right)\\[2ex] \Rightarrow & \qquad\qquad\quad\ \left(\begin{array}{cc} 4 & -2 \\ 4 & -2 \end{array}\right)\left(\begin{array}{c} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{array}\right) &=& \left(\begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 0\end{array}\right)\\[2ex] \Rightarrow & \qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad 4x_1 - 2x_2 &=& 0. \end{eqnarray*}\]

       Hence any vector of the form \(\displaystyle s\left(\begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 2\end{array}\right)\) is an eigenvector,

       (where \(s\ne0\) is an arbitrary constant.)

    • Case \(\lambda = 1\):
      Write out
      \(\displaystyle \qquad\qquad\qquad \left({\bf{A}} - \lambda{\bf{I}}\right)\boldsymbol{x} = {\bf{0}} \)
      in full to give

    \[\begin{eqnarray*} &\left[\left(\begin{array}{rr} 3 & -2 \\ 4 & -3 \end{array}\right) - \left(\begin{array}{cc} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{array}\right)\right]\left(\begin{array}{c} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{array}\right) &=& \left(\begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 0\end{array}\right)\\[2ex] \Rightarrow & \qquad\qquad\quad\ \left(\begin{array}{cc} 2 & -2 \\ 4 & -4 \end{array}\right)\left(\begin{array}{c} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{array}\right) &=& \left(\begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 0\end{array}\right)\\[2ex] \Rightarrow & \qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad x_1 - x_2 &=& 0. \end{eqnarray*}\]

       Hence any vector of the form \(\displaystyle t\left(\begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 1\end{array}\right)\) is an eigenvector,

       (where \(t\ne0\) is an arbitrary constant.)
