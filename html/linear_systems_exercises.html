Exercises: Problem Sheet 1

1.14. Exercises: Problem Sheet 1#

  1. Using Gaussian elimination, find the solutions to the following systems of equations

\[\begin{eqnarray*} (\textrm{a})\qquad & \phantom{-}x &+& 6y &+& z &=& \phantom{-}0 &\qquad\qquad& (\textrm{b}) \qquad & x &-& y &+& z &=& \phantom{-}1 \\ & \phantom{-}x &-& 4y &+& 2z &=& -2 && & x &+& y && &=& \phantom{-}6 \\ & -x &-& 2y &+& z &=& \phantom{-}2 && & x &-& 5y &+& 3z &=& -9 \end{eqnarray*}\]

  1. Using Gaussian elimination, find the solutions to the following systems of equations

\[\begin{eqnarray*} (\textrm{a})\qquad & 2x &+& 3y &-& z &=& 0 &\qquad\qquad& (\textrm{b}) \qquad & 4x &+& y &+& 5z &=& 0 \\ & x &-& 2y &+& 5z &=& 0 && & \phantom{4}x &+& 2y &-& 4z &=& 0 \\ & 3x &-& y &-& 2z &=& 0 && & -x &-& y &+& \phantom{4}z &=& 0 \end{eqnarray*}\]

[Hint: clearly \(x = y = z = 0\) is a solution to both systems of equations. Using Gaussian elimination you should be able to say whether either system of equations has any other solutions, besides \(x = y = z = 0\), or whether this solution is unique.]

  1. Find, where they exist, the matrices
    \({\bf{A}} + {\bf{B}}\), \({\bf{A}} - {\bf{B}}\), \({\bf{A}}{\bf{B}}\), \({\bf{B}}{\bf{A}}\), \(\left({\bf{A}} + {\bf{B}}\right)\left({\bf{A}} - {\bf{B}}\right)\), \({\bf{A}}^2 - {\bf{B}}^2\).

    • \(\displaystyle {\bf{A}} = \left(\begin{array}{rr} 4 & -5 \\ 2 & 0 \\ -6 & 3\end{array}\right) \qquad {\bf{B}} = \left(\begin{array}{rr} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & 7 \\ 2 & -5\end{array}\right)\)

    • \(\displaystyle {\bf{A}} = \left(\begin{array}{rrr} 2 & 3 & 4 \\ 1 & 5 & 6\end{array}\right) \qquad {\bf{B}} = \left(\begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3\end{array}\right)\)

    • \(\displaystyle {\bf{A}} = \left(\begin{array}{rr} 3 & -4 \\ 1 & 5 \\ -2 & 2\end{array}\right) \qquad {\bf{B}} = \left(\begin{array}{ccc} 1 & 2 & 1 \\ 4 & 0 & 2\end{array}\right)\)

    • \(\displaystyle {\bf{A}} = \left(\begin{array}{rrr} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ -4 & 0 & 6\end{array}\right) \qquad {\bf{B}} = \left(\begin{array}{rr} 2 & 6 \\ 8 & 4 \\ -1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0\end{array}\right)\)

    • \(\displaystyle {\bf{A}} = \left(\begin{array}{rrr} 2 & -1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 \\ 1 & 0 & 1\end{array}\right) \qquad {\bf{B}} = \left(\begin{array}{rrr} 0 & 3 & -5 \\ 2 & 0 & 6 \\ 1 & -1 & 4\end{array}\right)\)

  2. Given \(\displaystyle {\bf{A}} = \left(\begin{array}{rr} 1 & 1 \\ 2 & -1 \\ 0 & 3\end{array}\right)\), \(\displaystyle {\bf{B}} = \left(\begin{array}{rr} 0 & -3 \\ -1 & 2 \end{array}\right)\), and \(\displaystyle {\bf{C}} = \left(\begin{array}{rr} 0 & -5 \\ 3 & 2 \end{array}\right)\):

  • evaluate \(\left({\bf{A}}{\bf{B}}\right){\bf{C}}\) and \({\bf{A}}\left({\bf{B}}{\bf{C}}\right)\);

  • verify that \({\bf{A}}\left({\bf{B}} + {\bf{C}}\right) = {\bf{A}}{\bf{B}} + {\bf{A}}{\bf{C}}\);

  • find the matrix \({\bf{M}}\) such that \({\bf{B}}^3 + 3{\bf{C}}^2 + 2{\bf{M}} = {\bf{0}}\).

  1. If \(\displaystyle {\bf{A}} = \left(\begin{array}{rr} 3 & 2 \\ -2 & -1 \end{array}\right)\) and \({\bf{I}} = \left(\begin{array}{rr} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{array}\right)\), show that \(\left({\bf{A}} - {\bf{I}}\right)^2 = {\bf{0}}\).

  2. Calculate the following determinants

\[\begin{equation*} (\textrm{a})\qquad \left|\begin{array}{rr} -1 & 2 \\ 3 & -4 \end{array}\right|\qquad\qquad (\textrm{b})\qquad \left|\begin{array}{rr} 1 & -2 \\ -3 & 4 \end{array}\right|\qquad\qquad (\textrm{c})\qquad \left|\begin{array}{rr} 3 & -6 \\ -9 & 12 \end{array}\right| \end{equation*}\]

  1. Find the inverse of the matrix \(\displaystyle \left(\begin{array}{cc} 3 & 1 \\ 2 & 4 \end{array}\right)\) and hence solve

\[\begin{equation*} \left\{\begin{array}{rcl} 3x + \phantom{4}y \!\!&=&\!\! -2 \\ 2x + 4y \!\!&=&\!\! \phantom{-}3 \end{array}\right. \end{equation*}\]

  1. Show that the determinant \(\displaystyle \left|\begin{array}{rrr} 4 & -3 & 1 \\ 7 & 2 & -2 \\ 1 & -5 & 3 \end{array}\right|\) has the value 16 by:

  • expanding along the third row;

  • expanding along the second column.

  1. Show that the determinant of \(\displaystyle {\bf{A}} = \left(\begin{array}{ccc} t+3 & -1 & 1 \\ 5 & t-3 & 1 \\ 6 & -6 & t+4 \end{array}\right)\) is \(\left(t + 2\right)\left(t - 2\right)\left(t + 4\right)\).

[Hint: notice that the answer is nicely factorized. What method might you use to produce an answer in such a form?]

  1. Write each of the following systems of equations as an augmented matrix, and solve each system by applying elementary row operations to the augmented matrix.

(a)

\[\begin{eqnarray*} x\ &-& 2y &+& 2z &=& -3 \\ 2x\ &+& \phantom{2}y &-& 3z &=& \phantom{-}8 \\ -x\ &+& 3y &+& 2z &=& -5 \end{eqnarray*}\]

(b)

\[\begin{eqnarray*} x\ &-& 2y &+& 2z &=& -3 \\ 2x\ &+& \phantom{2}y &-& 3z &=& \phantom{-}8 \\ x\ &+& 8y &-& 12z &=& \,25 \end{eqnarray*}\]

(c)

\[\begin{eqnarray*} x\ &-& 2y &+& 2z &=& -3 \\ 2x\ &+& \phantom{2}y &-& 3z &=& \phantom{-}8 \\ 9x\ &-& 3y &-& 3z &=& \phantom{-}\alpha \end{eqnarray*}\]

   For what value of \(\alpha\) is there a non-trivial solution to (c)?

  1. Use the Gauss-Jordan method to invert the following matrices.

\[\begin{equation*} (\textrm{a})\quad {\bf{A}} = \left(\begin{array}{ccc} 2 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 \\ 3 & 1 & 0 \end{array}\right) \qquad (\textrm{b})\quad {\bf{B}} = \left(\begin{array}{rrr} 1 & -1 & 2 \\ 2 & -1 & 2 \\ 3 & 2 & -1 \end{array}\right) \end{equation*}\]

   In each case check your answer by calculating \({\bf{A}}{\bf{A}}^{-1}\).

  1. Find the eigenvalues and corresponding eigenvectors of the following matrices.

\[\begin{equation*} (\textrm{a})\quad \left(\begin{array}{rr} 7 & -2 \\ -2 & 4 \end{array}\right) \qquad (\textrm{b})\quad \left(\begin{array}{rrr} 1 & 2 & -1 \\ 2 & 1 & -1 \\ -1 & -1 & 2 \end{array}\right) \qquad (\textrm{c})\quad \left(\begin{array}{rrr} 5 & -6 & -6 \\ -1 & 4 & 2\\ 3 & -6 & -4 \end{array}\right) \end{equation*}\]

    • If \(\displaystyle {\bf{A}} = \left(\begin{array}{rrr} 2 & -1 & 0 \\ -1 & 2 & -1 \\ 0 & -1 & 2 \end{array}\right)\), confirm that the eigenvalues of \({\bf{A}}\) are \(\lambda_1 = 2\), \(\lambda_2 = 2 + \sqrt{2}\) and \(\lambda_3 = 2 - \sqrt{2}\).

    • Find \({\bf{A}}^2\) and calculate its eigenvalues. Confirm that they are \(\lambda_1^2\), \(\lambda_2^2\), and \(\lambda_3^2\).

    • Evaluate \(\textrm{det}\left({\bf{A}}\right)\), and show that \(\textrm{det}\left({\bf{A}}\right) = \lambda_1\lambda_2\lambda_3\).
