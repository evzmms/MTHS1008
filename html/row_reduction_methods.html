1.10. Row Reduction Methods#

  • The technique introduced in Week 2 (Introduction to Linear Systems, Further Linear Systems: 3 x 3 Systems) for solving systems of equations “Gaussian Elimination” can be applied using matrices

  • We note that the full equations need not be written out at each stage, only the numerical coefficients are required

  • These coefficients can be written in matrix form

The Augmented Matrix#

Suppose we wish to solve the following system of equations for unknowns \(x_1\), \(x_2\), and \(x_3\)

\[\begin{eqnarray*} 3x_1 \,&-&\, \phantom{3}x_2 \,&+&\, 2x_3 \,&=&\, \phantom{-}3 \\ 2x_1 \,&+&\, \phantom{3}x_2 \,&+&\, \phantom{2}x_3 \,&=&\, -2 \\ x_1 \,&-&\, 3x_2 && \,&=&\, \phantom{-}5 \end{eqnarray*}\]

In matrix form this is \({\bf{A}}\boldsymbol{x}=\boldsymbol{b}\), where

\[\begin{equation*} {\bf{A}} = \left(\begin{array}{rrr} 3 & -1 & 2 \\ 2 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -3 & 0 \end{array}\right), \quad \boldsymbol{x} = \left(\begin{array}{l} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3 \end{array}\right), \quad \boldsymbol{b} = \left(\begin{array}{r} 3 \\ -2 \\ 5 \end{array}\right). \end{equation*}\]

Definition 1.19 (Augmented Matrix)

We write down the augmented matrix \(\left[\bf{A}|\boldsymbol{b}\right]\) of coefficients and right-hand sides

\[\begin{equation*} \left[\begin{array}{rrr|r} 3 & -1 & 2 & 3 \\ 2 & 1 & 1 & -2 \\ 1 & -3 & 0 & 5 \end{array}\right] \end{equation*}\]

Gaussian Elimination#

  • Step 1
    We write down the augmented matrix of coefficients and right-hand sides

\[\begin{equation*} \left[\begin{array}{rrr|r} 3 & -1 & 2 & 3 \\ \color{red}2 & 1 & 1 & -2 \\ \color{red}1 & \color{red}-3 & 0 & 5 \end{array}\right] \end{equation*}\]

   We use row operations to carry out Gaussian elimination, making the terms below the leading diagonal

   (those in red) equal to zero.

   We do not need to write down equations for \(x_1\), \(x_2\), and \(x_3\) at each stage, we may simply deal with the

   numerical coefficients in the augmented matrix.

  • Step 2
    Remove the terms below the leading diagonal in the first column

\[\begin{equation*} \left[\begin{array}{rrr|r} 3 & -1 & 2 & 3 \\ \color{red}2 & 1 & 1 & -2 \\ \color{red}1 & -3 & 0 & 5 \end{array}\right] \end{equation*}\]
\[\begin{equation*} \left\{\begin{array}{l} \color{gray}R_1\rightarrow R_1 \\ R_2 \rightarrow 3R_2 - 2R_1 \\ R_3 \rightarrow 3R_3 - R_1 \end{array}\right. \qquad\qquad \left[\begin{array}{rrr|r} 3 & -1 & 2 & 3 \\ \color{red}0 & 5 & -1 & -12 \\ \color{red}0 & -8 & -2 & 12 \end{array}\right] \end{equation*}\]

  • Step 3
    Remove the terms below the leading diagonal in the first column

\[\begin{equation*} \left[\begin{array}{rrr|r} 3 & -1 & 2 & 3 \\ 0 & 5 & -1 & -12 \\ 0 & \color{red}-8 & -2 & 12 \end{array}\right] \end{equation*}\]
\[\begin{equation*} \left\{\begin{array}{l} \color{gray}R_1\rightarrow R_1 \\ \color{gray}R_2\rightarrow R_2 \\ R_3 \rightarrow 5R_3 + 8R_2 \end{array}\right. \qquad\qquad \left[\begin{array}{rrr|r} 3 & -1 & 2 & 3 \\ 0 & 5 & -1 & -12 \\ 0 & \color{red}0 & -18 & -36 \end{array}\right] \end{equation*}\]

  • Step 4 Rewrite each row as an equation for \(x_1\), \(x_2\), and \(x_3\).
    The last row gives us an equation for \(x_3\):

\[\begin{equation*} -18x_3 = -36. \end{equation*}\]

   Solving this equation gives \(x_3 = 2\).

  • Step 5 Substitute \(x_3\) into the equation from row 2:

\[\begin{equation*} 5x_2 - x_3 = -12 \quad\Rightarrow\quad 5x_2 = -12 + 2 = -10 \quad\Rightarrow\quad x_2 = -2. \end{equation*}\]

  • Step 6 Substitute \(x_2\) and \(x_3\) into the equation from row 1:

\[\begin{equation*} 3x_1 - x_2 + 2x_3 = 3 \quad\Rightarrow\quad 3x_1 = 3 - 2 - 4 = -3 \quad\Rightarrow\quad x_1 = -1. \end{equation*}\]

The operations in Steps 2 and 3 are examples of elementary row operations (EROs).

Definition 1.20 (Elementary Row Operations)

There are three types of elementary row operation

  1. interchange any two rows;
    This is equivalent to swapping the order of any two equations.

  2. multiply any row by any nonzero constant;
    This is equivalent to multiplying both sides of a given equation by the constant.

  3. add a multiple of one row to another;
    This is equivalent to adding a multiple of one equation to another.

Warning

  • It is important to distinguish EROs from the broader range of operations that may be applied to determinants

  • In particular, operations to columns are not allowed when solving a system of equations by EROs.
