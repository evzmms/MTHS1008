1.2. Further Linear Systems: 3 x 3 Systems#

A 3 x 3 System#

A similar procedure can be used to solve a system of three linear equations for three unknowns \(x_1\), \(x_2\), and \(x_3\).

For example, suppose we wish to solve

\[\begin{eqnarray*} 2x_1 &+& 3x_2 &-& x_3 &=& \phantom{-}5 \tag{1.8} \\ 4x_1 &+& 4x_2 &−& 3x_3 &=& \phantom{-}3 \tag{1.9} \\ 2x_1 &−& 3x_2 &+& x_3 &=& −1 \tag{1.10} \end{eqnarray*}\]

to obtain \(x_1\), \(x_2\), and \(x_3\). There are more steps involved because there are more equations and more unknowns.

  • Step 1
    Eliminate \(x_1\) from equations (1.9) and (1.10) by subtracting multiples of (1.8).
    Replace

\[\begin{align*} (1.9) &\rightarrow (1.9)−2\times(1.8) \\ (1.10) &\rightarrow (1.10)−(1.8) \end{align*}\]

   to leave the system

\[\begin{eqnarray*} 2x_1 &+& 3x_2 &-& x_3 &=& \phantom{-}5 \tag{1.11} \\ &−& 2x_2 &−& x_3 &=& −7 \tag{1.12} \\ &−& 6x_2 &+& 2x_3 &=& −6 \tag{1.13} \end{eqnarray*}\]

  • Step 2
    Eliminate \(x_2\) from (1.13). To do this, we subtract an appropriate multiple of (1.12) from (1.13).  (If we subtract a multiple of (1.11) from (1.13) instead, then in the process of eliminating \(x_2\) from (1.13) we reintroduce \(x_1\)!)

   Replace

\[(1.13) \rightarrow (1.13) −3\times(1.12)\]

   to leave

\[\begin{eqnarray*} 2x_1 &+& 3x_2 &−& x_3 &=& \phantom{-}5 \tag{1.14} \\ &−& 2x_2 &−& x_3 &=& −7 \tag{1.15} \\ && && 5x_3 &=& \phantom{-}15 \tag{1.16} \end{eqnarray*}\]

  • Step 3
    Solve the “\(1\times1\) system” (1.16) to find \(x_3\).  In this case there is a unique solution to (1.16)

\[x_3 = \frac{15}{5} = 3.\]

  • Step 4
    Substitute this result back into (1.15) to give \(x_2\) from

\[−2x_2 − 3 = −7 \quad\Rightarrow\quad 2x_2 = 4 \quad\Rightarrow\quad x_2 = 2.\]

  • Step 5
    Substitute the values we have found for \(x_2\) and \(x_3\) back into (1.14) to give

\[2x_1 + 6 − 3 = 5 \quad\Rightarrow\quad 2x_1 = 2 \quad\Rightarrow\quad x_1 = 1.\]

   Finally, the solution to the system (1.8), (1.9), (1.10), is

\[x_1 = 1, \quad x_2 = 2, \quad x_3 = 3.\]

   This solution is unique.

Definition 1.1 (Gaussian Elimination)

This method is known as Gaussian Elimination and can be applied to any system where the number of equations equals the number of unknowns.

Gaussian Elimination in 3 x 3 Systems#

We apply Gaussian elimination to the equations

\[\begin{eqnarray*} x_1 &+& x_2 &+& 2x_3 &=& 1 \tag{$R_1$} \\ 2x_1 &−& x_2 &−& x_3 &=& 1 \tag{$R_2$} \\ x_1 &+& 4x_2 &+& 7x_3 &=& 2 \tag{$R_3$} \end{eqnarray*}\]

using more compact notation.

  • Step 1
    Eliminate \(x_1\) from the last two equations

\[\begin{eqnarray*} &&&& & x_1 &+& ~x_2 &+& 2x_3 &= 1 \tag{$R_1'$}\\ R_2 &\rightarrow& R_2 &-& 2R_1 \qquad & &−& 3x_2 &−& 5x_3 &= −1 \tag{$R_2'$}\\ R_3 &\rightarrow& R_3 &-& R_1 \qquad & & & 3x_2 &+& 5x_3 &= 1 \tag{$R_3'$} \end{eqnarray*}\]

  • Step 2
    Eliminate \(x_2\) from the last equation (use \(R_2'\) so \(x_1\) doesn’t come back!)

\[\begin{eqnarray*} &&&& & x_1 &+& ~x_2 &+& 2x_3 &= 1 \tag{$R_1''$}\\ &&&& & &−& 3x_2 &−& 5x_3 &= −1 \tag{$R_2''$}\\ R_3' &\rightarrow& R_3' &+& R_2' \qquad &&&&& 0x_3 &= 0 \tag{$R_3''$} \end{eqnarray*}\]

  • Step 3
    The last equation is a “\(1\times1\) system” which is easily solved. In this case the equation

\[0 x_3= 0\]

   is satisfied for any value of \(x_3\) and there infinitely many solutions.

   Let

\[x_3 = t,\]

   where \(t\) is an arbitrary number.

  • Step 4
    Substitute \(x_3\) back into the second equation \(R_2''\) to get

\[−3x_2 − 5x_3 = −3x_2 − 5t = −1 \quad\Rightarrow\quad x_2 = \frac{1}{3} − \frac{5t}{3}.\]

  • Step 5
    Substitute \(x_2\) and \(x_3\) back into the first equation \(R_1''\) to get

\[x_1 + x_2 + 2x_3 = x_1 + \frac{1}{3} − \frac{5t}{3} + 2t = 1 \quad\Rightarrow\quad x_1 = \frac{2}{3} − \frac{t}{3}.\]

Conclusion
For any given value of \(t\)

\[\begin{align*} x_1 &= \frac{2}{3} - \frac{t}{3}, \\ x_2 &= \frac{1}{3} − \frac{5t}{3}, \\ x_3 &= t, \end{align*}\]

is a solution of the original set of equations and there are an infinite number of such solutions.

Exercise

Check this conclusion by direct substitution in the original equations R1, R2, and R3.

3 x 3 Systems#

  • Equations of the form \(a x_1 + b x_2 = u\) represent lines in two-dimensional space.

  • Equations of the form \(a x_1 + b x_2 + cx_3 = u\) represent planes in three-dimensional space.

  • Systems of 3 equations in 3 unknowns represent 3 planes in three-dimensional space. We still have the three possible outcomes of: unique solution, no solution, infinitely many solutions.

  • All three planes intersecting at a single point - a unique solution

The image above illustrates equations (1.8), (1.9), and (1.10).

  • The three planes do not interstect at any point in space - no solution
    There is not a point in space that lies on the white, green and blue plane.

  • The three planes intersect in a line - infinitely many solutions
    The image shown below is the set of equations \(R_1\), \(R_2\), and \(R_3\) solved above.

  • The three planes intersect in a plane (they are all the same plane) - infinitely many solutions

Example 1.4

Use the method of Gaussian elimination to solve

\[\begin{eqnarray*} 2x_1 &-& x_2 &+& x_3 &=& \phantom{-}8 \\ 4x_1 &+& x_2 &+& x_3 &=& \phantom{-}6 \\ x_1 &−& 3x_2 &-& 2x_3 &=& −1 \end{eqnarray*}\]

Solution

\[\begin{equation*} x_1 = 1, \quad x_2 = -2, \quad x_3 = 4. \end{equation*}\]

Example 1.5

Use the method of Gaussian elimination to solve

\[\begin{eqnarray*} x_1 &+& 2x_2 &+& 3x_3 &=& 3 \\ 2x_1 &-& x_2 &-& 4x_3 &=& 1 \\ x_1 &+& x_2 &+& x_3 &=& 2 \end{eqnarray*}\]

Solution

\[\begin{equation*} x_1 = 1 + t, \quad x_2 = 1 - 2t, \quad x_3 = t. \end{equation*}\]

Note that there is not a unique form for the answer here. Other forms of the solution that work include

\[\begin{equation*} x_1 = \frac{1}{2}(3 - t), \quad x_2 = t, \quad x_3 = \frac{1}{2}(1-t) \quad\textrm{or }\ldots \end{equation*}\]
\[\begin{equation*} x_1 = t, \quad x_2 = 3 - 2t, \quad x_3 = -1 + t \quad\textrm{or }\ldots \end{equation*}\]
\[\begin{equation*} x_1 = 1 + 2s, \quad x_2 = 1 - 4s, \quad x_3 = 2s \quad\textrm{etc }\ldots \end{equation*}\]
